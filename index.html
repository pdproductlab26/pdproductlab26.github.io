Optikon ↗ optikon.dev Finance Private Real

You're spending big on AI. Can your business see the return? Real-time cost allocation and variance detection across every LLM provider, every dollar traced to an owner.

AI FinOps
↗ My Substack: Your Newest Employees Work All Night. Nobody Knows If They Earn Their Keep.
The term optikon (often spelled opticon) derives from the Greek root word meaning "of or for sight".

Most enterprises receive AI spend as a single line on an invoice. Optikon insists that if a dollar was spent, you can say whose hand spent it.

Optikon commits to one promise: every AI dollar must leave a trace. Not a summary. Not a category. A trace, attributable, timestamped, owned, explainable.
What it is
  • AI spend visibility and allocation tool for FinOps teams: real-time cost allocation, variance detection, and governance across every LLM provider an enterprise uses.
  • Pulls spend from OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, and every other AI API. Maps every dollar to a cost center, team, use case, and owner.
  • Most enterprises today receive AI spend as a single-line invoice with no decomposition, no attribution, and no governance.
  • Optikon eliminates all of that.
The core problem
  • Finance teams are discovering AI spend at month-end close, weeks after the variance compounded, long after any corrective action is possible.
  • The AI invoice arrives as one number. It does not say which team, which product, which use case, or which model.
  • One enterprise ran up $500M in Claude charges before anyone noticed.
  • This is not a tooling gap. It is a visibility gap: the total absence of a ledger between what AI providers charge and what finance can explain.
Real-time cost allocationEvery AI dollar mapped to a cost center, team, use case, and provider, updated continuously. Budget-to-actual you can trust between closes.
Variance alertsAnomalies flagged against rolling forecast. Alerts land in time to act before the variance compounds. Unattributed spend surfaced in the same flow.
Unit economicsCost per request, cost per use case, model cost comparison. Find where you can save without sacrificing quality.
Governance and auditBudget enforcement per cost center with spend-threshold alerts. Finance-ready chargeback and showback exports showing AI spend by business unit, period-over-period, the allocation model FinOps teams actually need.

The buyer's objection

"We already have Datadog. We have our cloud cost management in Apptio. We track OpenAI spend in our billing dashboard. What exactly does Optikon do that we don't already have?"

The answer

  • The tools FinOps teams already use are observability stacks. They tell you what happened.
  • They do not answer: who is responsible, does this match the forecast, and what should change.
  • Those questions live entirely unmapped in the existing stack. Optikon owns them.
Attribution layer, not measurement layer
  • Datadog tells engineering the call was made. It cannot tell finance who pays for it. Attribution requires: API key to cost center mapping, a budget baseline FinOps owns, and real-time comparison with alert routing. No existing tool provides all three.
Continuous, not monthly
  • The root cause of invoice shock is that every existing tool operates on daily or monthly summaries. The variance compounds for weeks before it is visible. Optikon updates continuously.
Org structure, not cloud accounts
  • Finance thinks in cost centers. Datadog, Apptio, and cloud billing tools use different primitives. Optikon maintains the mapping layer between them, configurable, applied retroactively.
Unit economics, not spend totals
  • Knowing a cost center spent a given amount last month is useful. Knowing the cost per ticket for a use case, and that a cheaper model would cut it materially, is actionable.
Every cost management tool you have was built for cloud infrastructure: they can tell you what your AI APIs charged, but not which team, which use case, or whether it matched the budget. Optikon is the allocation layer that maps every AI dollar to a cost center and alerts you before the variance becomes an invoice.
Three intelligence layers

Provider Ingestion

Route traffic through the Optikon gateway or connect per-team keys. Pulls usage and spend from OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, and more, near real-time. Token counts by model, by project, by API key.

Allocation Engine

Raw spend mapped automatically to the enterprise's cost center tree: team, product, environment, use case. Rules configurable and applied retroactively. Unattributed spend surfaced and routed to owners for confirmation.

Variance & Governance

Budget baselines per cost center. Statistical spike detection vs. rolling 30-day baseline. Real-time comparison against forecast. Budget enforcement on overruns, with alerts that land in time to act.

Ichnos ↗ ichnos.dev Development Private Real

A flight recorder for regulated AI code. Every AI-written change leaves a tamper-evident record, kept in git and preserved in an independent system of record, so when regulators ask why, you already have the receipt.

AI Provenance (Regulatory)
ichnos (ίχνος) is ancient Greek for a track, a footprint - the physical mark left by an agent that passed through a space.

Your AI assistant has already written code, reviewed PRs, and proposed architecture. None of it left a mark.

Ichnos captures the decision record for every AI-written change at the moment it is made: timestamped, kept in git alongside the code it produced, and preserved in an independent, server-side system of record that is re-verified on demand, so any later edit to the trail is detectable.

AI-accountability expectations are tightening, such as the EU AI Act and California AB 2013. When your auditor asks what the AI decided and why, ichnos is your answer.
What it is
  • Compliance infrastructure layer for AI-generated code. Captures, automatically, with a verifiable integrity hash, stored in git, the provenance, decision context, assumptions, and human disposition behind every AI-written change - starting with Claude Code (Cursor and GitHub Copilot on the roadmap).
  • A large and growing share of production code is already AI-authored, and none of it shows its work: no record of what the model chose, what it assumed, what it skipped, or which human reviewed it.
  • AI-accountability expectations tighten through 2026, with regulators and customers increasingly asking what the AI decided and why.
  • Almost no engineering team can produce that record today. Ichnos eliminates all of that.
The core problem
  • An AI coding assistant produces a change. Six months later, in a production incident: why did we use polling instead of webhooks? Why did we skip the idempotency retry?
  • There is no answer, not in git history, not in the PR description, not anywhere. The model made those choices in milliseconds and wrote none of them down.
  • Multiply that across a large and growing share of the codebase. The result: a growing fraction of decisions that are permanently unrecoverable, invisible during maintenance, invisible to the regulator.
At-generation decision captureFires before the engineer sees the diff. The decision record is written when generation context is still present. There is no later point at which this information is recoverable.
Permanent records in your own repositoryDecision records are committed into your repository alongside the code and preserved in an independent, server-side system of record, so the trail can be re-verified on demand and cannot be quietly rewritten.
Human oversight evidence per changeaccepted / accepted_with_modifications / rejected; AI accountability frameworks require evidence of human oversight per change. This is that record, timestamped and linked to its decision payload.
Provenance exportgit why log --export provenance-report produces a structured provenance report: AI system identification, full decision index, human oversight summary, integrity manifest.

The buyer's objection

"We already have GitHub Copilot. We have Snyk scanning our PRs. Our engineers write PR descriptions. We have a code review process. We even have Datadog tracing our services in production. What exactly does Ichnos do that we don't already have?"

The answer

  • Every tool in that list was designed to answer one question and stop. None were designed to answer the question regulators are now asking.
  • "What did the AI produce when it wrote this code, and what did a human decide to do with it?"
  • The PR description documents what the engineer intended. It does not document what the AI assumed, what it considered and rejected, or what it explicitly skipped.
Generation time, not review time
  • Every existing tool operates after the code has been written. By that point, the generation context is gone. Ichnos fires at the PostToolUse boundary, before the engineer sees the diff.
Human layer, not code layer
  • The disposition layer records what the human decided to do with the AI's output. This is what AI accountability frameworks require. A GitHub PR approval is not a provenance record.
Permanent record, independently verifiable
  • Decision records are kept in git alongside the code, so the trail lives with your repository and outlives any single vendor. The same records are preserved in an independent, server-side system of record, re-verified on demand so the trail cannot be quietly rewritten.
Structured for accountability, not proprietary
  • Every other tool produces output in its own format. Ichnos ships a structured provenance report as a first-class export. No manual assembly, no translation layer needed.
Every tool you have watches what the code does: Copilot writes it, Snyk scans it, reviewers read it, Datadog monitors it. Ichnos is the layer that records what the AI decided when it wrote it, what the human chose to do with that decision, and hands you a verifiable provenance report when the auditor asks, while your engineers keep working exactly as they do today.
Three capture layers

Capture at the moment of creation

Fires after every AI code edit in Claude Code, before the engineer sees the diff. Model identifier, timestamp, file path, decision payload, captured at the moment of generation.

Engineer decision record

Records what the engineer did with the AI output: accepted, accepted with modifications, or rejected. The evidence that a person reviewed the change and made a deliberate decision.

Provenance reporting

AI decisions, human oversight evidence, integrity manifest. One command: git why log --export provenance-report. Delivered. No manual assembly.

AI Project Intelligence
Alethinos (ἀληθινός) is an ancient Greek word for "true," but not true in the ordinary sense. It means the genuine article as opposed to its shadow. True light, not a reflection of light. The real thing, not a copy.

Jira gives you the shadow of your project: a list of what exists, frozen at the moment someone last updated it. Behind that list is usually a department full of people doing stitch-and-join by hand, pulling status from standups, Slack threads, PRs, and spreadsheets, then typing it back in. The list is never current. It is always a representation of what was true.

Alethinos gives you what is actually true right now, derived from every signal the project is emitting as signals arrive. No one updates it. It updates itself.
What it is
  • AI project intelligence platform that sits on top of Jira and tells engineering organisations, in plain English, why their deliverables are at risk and what to do about it, weeks before deadlines slip.
  • Reads three signal layers Jira ignores: structured ticket data, human language in comments, and code signals in connected repositories.
  • Cascades intelligence bottom-up, story to epic to deliverable, and produces a real forecast against every committed date, with cited root causes and recommended actions.
  • Every Monday morning, PMO teams spend hours manually assembling this picture from Jira exports, Slack threads, and gut feel. Alethinos eliminates all of that.
The core problem
  • A delivery manager opens a spreadsheet. Exports stories. Manually tags each one red, amber, or green based on gut feel.
  • If a story slipped because an engineer mentioned in a comment that she'd been pulled onto a production incident, the delivery manager almost certainly missed it.
  • Jira recorded the comment. Nobody read it.
  • The VP's deck is wrong. The committed date is a fiction. And nobody knows until the sprint ends.

Natural language queries"Why is the Authentication overhaul 22 days behind?" returns a cited, plain-English answer referencing specific tickets, comments, and engineers.
Scenario modellingWhat happens to the Q3 deliverable if we add one engineer to this epic? Modelled against real velocity data, not planning-poker guesses.

"We have Jira. We have Advanced Roadmaps. Our delivery managers do weekly status updates. We have dashboards in Confluence. What exactly does Alethinos do that we don't already have?"

The answer

  • It does not read comments, recompute forecast dates when stories change, or detect that an engineer hasn't committed code in five days.
  • The gap between a Jira record and an accurate delivery forecast is filled by hours of manual human work every week. Alethinos closes that gap.
Computation, not display
  • Alethinos computes data no human entered. The cascade date engine derives the correct forecast from story-level actuals, every time anything changes.

Explanations, not metrics
  • Every root cause has an evidence quote. Every health score has a cited reason. A briefing to act on, not a chart to interpret.
Every tool in your stack records what happened. Alethinos is the system that reads your tickets, comments, and code simultaneously to tell you why your projects are actually behind and what to do about it, before your next sprint review.

Structured Jira Data

Projects, epics, stories, velocity: the mathematically correct forecast date computed bottom-up on each sync. Not what someone entered. What is actually true.

Agentic DevSecOps
Aegis in Greek refers to the protection, backing, or sponsorship of a powerful individual, organization, or governing body.

That is what this product commits to: not a faster alert, but a different relationship to threat entirely.

Aegis does not wait for the CVE to ship or the secret to leak or the misconfiguration to get flagged in production. It intercepts at the moment of creation - the PR, the pipeline run, the Terraform plan - and closes the exposure before it becomes a breach.

Every feature decision in Aegis must answer one question: does this make the threat unreachable, or does it just make the alert louder?

Before the breach finds you.
What it is
  • Agentic DevSecOps platform that monitors code repositories, CI/CD pipelines, infrastructure-as-code, and container images in real time - and doesn't just report what it finds, but acts on it.
  • Opens pull requests to rotate exposed secrets, blocks merges on critical CVEs, patches IaC misconfigurations before deployment, and produces compliance evidence automatically.
  • Patches lag for weeks after disclosure, and committed secrets sit unrotated long before anyone notices.
  • Security teams today operate a fragmented stack of scanners that fire alerts into a void. Aegis eliminates all of that.

  • Security engineers at mid-to-large engineering organisations are running five to nine discrete tools - each producing its own alert queue that feeds into a Jira backlog nobody has time to triage.
  • IaC misconfigurations are caught at deploy time if they are caught at all. When the SOC 2 auditor arrives, someone spends three weeks manually compiling evidence that the pipeline controls were operating.
  • No single tool owns the remediation loop. Every tool reports; nothing fixes.
  • The security team is a triage queue masquerading as a function. Aegis ends this.

Pistis treats every grant as a standing claim: the claim holds, access holds; the claim fails, access is revoked - automatically, without a ticket, without a quarterly review.

SailPoint and Okta certify that someone approved an entitlement. Pistis certifies it is still warranted right now.
